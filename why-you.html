On Timing
Everyone Arrives at a
Crossroads

If you're reading this, there's a reason you were invited. Not because of your CV. Not because of your LinkedIn profile. Because somewhere in your life right now, there's a version of you that knows things are shifting.

Maybe you can feel it. Maybe you've been feeling it for a while. The career path that made sense at 22 is starting to look different at 32, or 42, or 52. The job title that felt like achievement is starting to feel like a ceiling. The company you work for is starting to feel less certain about its own future than you are about yours.

The world is changing at a speed that is making the traditional career script feel outdated. AI is not going to replace skilled, creative, experienced people. But it is going to make the right team of skilled, creative, experienced people capable of building something that used to require 200 people and £10 million in funding.

The crossroads is real. The question is what you do at it.

What That Crossroads Often Looks Like
  • 01
    You're good at what you do — really good — but the organisation you're doing it for doesn't seem to care as much as you do. You know you're capable of more than they're giving you room for.
  • 02
    You've been thinking about doing something for yourself for years. The idea never quite goes away. But the risk always feels too large, the timing never quite right, and you don't know who you'd do it with.
  • 03
    You look at what AI is doing to your industry and you feel one of two things: threatened or curious. If you're in this room, you're probably more curious than threatened. That instinct is worth something.
  • 04
    You've got domain expertise, relationships, or a way of seeing the world that you know is valuable — but you've been giving it to someone else's company for someone else's benefit for a long time.
  • 05
    You've been waiting for the right opportunity with the right people. Not just a job. Not just a project. Something you'd actually want to be remembered for being part of.
On Why You Specifically
I Didn't Open a
Job Board

Building a company the conventional way means writing job descriptions, posting them, filtering applications, interviewing strangers, and hoping the person you hire actually cares about what you're building. Most of the time, they don't. Not really. They care about the salary, the title, the commute, and whether the team seems decent enough. That's rational. It's also not enough.

What we're building requires a different kind of team. People who already know what they're worth. Who bring something specific that they've spent years developing. Who don't need managing — they need direction and then room to operate.

The people in this room were chosen before a single slide was written. Not because you're the most qualified people on paper. Because I know what you're capable of. Because I've seen it. Because I trust you in a way that takes most companies years to build — and most companies never quite manage.

That trust is the foundation of everything we're about to talk about. The business is compelling. The numbers are real. The timing is right. But none of that matters if the people aren't right. The people in this room are right. That's why you're here.

🎥
We Are Filming This.

Because in five years, when people ask how this started — this is the scene. A room full of people who said yes before anyone else believed it. The part of the documentary everyone rewinds. The moment before everything changed.

We're not filming it because we're certain it'll be worth watching. We're filming it because the people who build the things worth watching always wish they had.

On Honesty
What We Are Not Asking

We are not asking you to quit your job tomorrow. We are not asking you to put your mortgage at risk. We are not asking you to bet everything on something you heard about for the first time today.

We are asking you to honestly consider what you could bring to this — and whether what you could bring would be worth owning a piece of what it becomes.

There are different levels of involvement. Full-time. Part-time. Project-based. A few hours a week doing something only you can do. Every level is real. Every level is valued. Every level gets equity — a genuine piece of ownership in what we're building.

And if the answer is no — that's genuinely fine. Not every opportunity is right for every person at every time. The world is full of good opportunities. This happens to be a great one, at a rare moment, with the right people. But you are the only person who can decide whether it's yours.

If you're not in, we part as friends and the respect between us doesn't move an inch. This conversation never happened if you need it to never have happened.

One Week From Now
Your Opening Chapter

You have a week. Not to decide yes or no — that pressure isn't on you yet. To think about what you'd bring to this, if you were in.

When you come back, bring three things. Not a CV. Not a formal pitch. Just three honest answers that only you could give. Think of it as the first thing you'd contribute — the opening paragraph of your chapter in this story.

1
Your Role
What would you build here?
Not a job title. The actual thing. The work only you would do, the problems only you'd see, the doors only you could open. Where does your specific combination of experience, relationships, and instinct fit into what you just heard?
2
Your Commitment
How much of yourself can you give?
Honestly. Not what you think sounds impressive — what is actually real right now. Full-time, evenings and weekends, 5 hours a week on a specific thing. The honest answer is the right answer. Everything is structured around reality, not aspiration.
3
Your Idea
One thing only you'd bring.
One idea. One connection. One angle. One thing that occurred to you during this presentation that we haven't thought of yet — or that you'd do differently. The thing that reminds us why we invited you specifically and not someone else.
This is not an application process. There are no right answers and no wrong ones. Nobody is being assessed against a rubric or competing against each other. This is just the beginning of an honest conversation about what you'd contribute — so we can figure out together where you fit and what that's worth. The people who come back are the ones who built the thing. The ones who come back with something real are the ones who own the most of it.
The World Is
Big.

There are a thousand paths from here. Most of them are perfectly good paths. This is one path — and it happens to be the one I'm most excited about, with the people I'd most want to walk it with.

But if there's a version of you that felt something in that room today — that recognised something in what you heard — I'd encourage you to listen to that version.

That version usually knows what it's doing.

Take the week. Come back with something real.
